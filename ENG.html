Key Questions

Today, we answer
three questions

The key is to view a data center not simply as an IT building, but as infrastructure that combines power, cooling, connectivity, and mission-critical operations.

1

Why is a data center different from conventional commercial real estate?

Unlike an office or warehouse, value is driven less by space alone and more by the ability to deliver reliable power, cooling, and network connectivity.

2

How is AI changing data center design?

AI servers consume far more power and generate far more heat, shifting design priorities from larger floor area to higher power density and greater cooling capacity.

3

Where do operating risks arise?

Power failures, cooling outages, network disruptions, maintenance errors, and human error can all lead to service interruptions.

AI Servers and Rack Power Density

In AI infrastructure,
GPUs dominate power demand and heat output

Traditional enterprise servers were centered on CPUs and storage. In AI systems, GPUs are the primary driver of electrical demand and heat output. As GPU power rises, conventional air cooling alone becomes increasingly difficult at the rack level.

At 30 kW, 60 kW, 100 kW and beyond per rack, power delivery, distribution, cooling, structural loading, piping, and maintenance practices all change.

Part 2
Cooling Design

As heat density rises, cooling moves
from air toward liquid

Data center cooling can be grouped into air cooling, direct liquid cooling, and immersion cooling. Hot-aisle/cold-aisle layouts and containment are airflow-management strategies within air-cooled environments, not separate cooling categories.

1. Air Cooling

Conditioned air is supplied to server intakes and hot exhaust air is removed from the rear. Hot-aisle/cold-aisle layouts and containment reduce air mixing and improve efficiency.

2. Direct Liquid Cooling

A liquid loop is brought close to high-heat components such as GPUs and CPUs to remove heat directly. This is becoming the most practical next step for AI-ready facilities.

3. Immersion Cooling

IT equipment is submerged in a dielectric fluid. It can support extremely high heat densities but requires changes to hardware standards, maintenance, and operating procedures.

Cushman & Wakefield

Part 2
Where Korea's Data Center Cooling Market Stands

Korea is currently
transitioning from optimized air cooling to liquid cooling

Most operating facilities in Korea still rely on precision air conditioning, hot-aisle/cold-aisle layouts, containment, and economization. However, AI demand is accelerating planning and deployment discussions for liquid cooling to support high-density GPU racks.

Korea's hot, humid summers and cold, dry winters make year-round direct-air economization impractical. Mechanical cooling and supplemental systems are still required across portions of the year.

Current Cooling Baseline
Precision air cooling, hot-aisle/cold-aisle layouts, and containment remain the prevailing operating model.
Cooling Efficiency Optimization
Facilities are expanding the use of air-side or water-side economization and other climate-responsive strategies to reduce mechanical cooling demand.
Transition to Liquid Cooling
For AI and GPU deployments, liquid cooling is emerging as the next practical design requirement for supporting high-density racks.

Liquid Cooling, Simplified

For AI data centers,
liquid coolingis becoming a core design issue

Liquid cooling brings coolant close to high-heat components such as GPUs and CPUs. It supports higher rack densities than air cooling, but adds piping, CDUs, leak detection, commissioning, and maintenance responsibilities.

Advantages

  • Supports high-density GPU racks
  • Can reduce mechanical cooling demand
  • Enables higher power density per rack

Key Considerations

  • Additional capital cost for piping, CDUs, and controls
  • Leak and maintenance risk
  • Clear demarcation of operator and customer responsibilities

Part 2
Systems Required for Liquid Cooling

Liquid cooling is
not a single piece of equipment—
it is an integrated cooling architecture

Even in a liquid-cooled deployment, not all IT equipment is liquid cooled. GPUs and CPUs may use direct liquid cooling, while network equipment and other components may still require air cooling.

Future facilities are therefore likely to operate as hybrid environments combining air and liquid cooling.

Warm-water loopSupplies relatively warm water to the CDU, which can reduce chiller dependence and improve energy performance.
Low-temperature chilled-water loopCRAC/CRAH units, electrical rooms, and some air-cooled equipment may still require lower-temperature chilled water.
Technology cooling loopThe secondary coolant network from the CDU to racks and servers. Leak detection, isolation, and maintainability are critical.
Continuous CoolingDuring a utility outage, UPS-backed pumps, thermal inertia in the piping, and buffer tanks can reduce the cooling gap.

Keeping IT power online is not enough—
cooling must also ride through

UPS systems and generators can maintain electrical service during an outage, but chillers, pumps, and fans may require time to restart. The cooling system therefore needs ride-through capability.

Data Center Glossary

Data Center Fundamentals

Data Center A mission-critical facility that houses IT equipment and provides the power, cooling, connectivity, security, and operational support required for continuous service.
Server A computing system that processes data and runs applications, cloud services, and AI workloads.
Storage Hardware or systems used to retain and retrieve digital data.
Network Equipment Switches, routers, and related hardware that connect servers to one another and to external networks.
Rack A standardized cabinet used to mount servers, network equipment, and related IT hardware.
White Space The customer-facing data hall area where racks and IT equipment are installed.
Data Hall A secure room or operating zone containing multiple rows of IT racks.
Mechanical, Electrical and Plumbing (MEP) The base-building and mission-critical systems that provide electrical power, heat rejection, air or liquid cooling, and water distribution.
Meet-me Room (MMR) A carrier-neutral interconnection room where telecommunications providers and customers cross-connect.

Commercial Models and Real Estate Terms

Colocation A service model in which customers lease data hall space, power, cooling, connectivity, security, and operational support from a third-party operator.
Retail Colocation Rack, cage, or small-suite colocation serving multiple customers within a shared facility.
Wholesale Colocation Large capacity blocks, dedicated data halls, or suites leased to enterprise or hyperscale customers.
Cloud Service Provider (CSP) A company that provides cloud computing services; large providers are commonly referred to as hyperscalers.
Enterprise Customer A corporate user that deploys its own IT environment in a third-party data center.
Build-to-Suit (BTS) A facility developed to the technical, capacity, and delivery requirements of a specific customer.
Build-to-Order / Build-to-Operate (BTO) A customer-specific development or operating structure; in U.S. data center transactions, build-to-suit is generally the more common term.
Powered Shell A data center shell with utility service and base-building infrastructure in place, while portions of the technical fit-out are completed by the customer.
Service Level Agreement (SLA) A contract that defines committed service levels and remedies, including uptime, power availability, environmental conditions, and response or restoration times.

Electrical Infrastructure Terms 1/2

IT Load The electrical load consumed directly by servers, storage, and network equipment.
Facility Load The total site load, including IT load and supporting infrastructure such as cooling, lighting, pumps, and electrical losses.
Power Receiving The delivery of utility electrical service to the site; commonly described in the U.S. as utility service or utility interconnection.
Power Receiving Capacity The amount of utility power available or contractually deliverable to the site; commonly described as available utility capacity.
Substation Electrical infrastructure that transforms and distributes power at the voltage required by the facility.
Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS) A battery-backed system that provides immediate ride-through power and power conditioning during utility interruptions.
Generator A standby engine-generator that supports the facility during an extended utility outage.
Power Distribution Unit (PDU) Equipment that distributes electrical power to racks or downstream IT loads.
Static Transfer Switch (STS) A solid-state device that rapidly transfers a load between independent power sources.
Automatic Transfer Switch (ATS) A switching device that automatically transfers loads between normal and standby sources.

Cooling Infrastructure Terms 1/2

Cooling The process of removing IT heat and rejecting it outside the facility.
Air Cooling A cooling method that removes IT heat through conditioned airflow.
Hot Aisle / Cold Aisle A rack layout that separates cold supply aisles from hot exhaust aisles.

Sustainability and Efficiency Terms
